# Module 4: Technical Screens

The technical screen is the round that makes strong engineers nervous, because it feels like a test of
whether you can solve the problem in time. It is not. It is a test of how you think while you solve it.
The interviewer is watching a process: do you clarify before you code, do you narrate your reasoning, do
you stay coherent when you hit a bug, do you test your own work. The code is the residue; the thinking is
the signal.

That reframing is the whole module. A candidate who silently produces a correct solution scores below a
candidate who talks through a slightly messier one, because the silent candidate gave the interviewer
nothing to evaluate except the final state. The skill this module builds is the communication layer: the
running narration that turns a coding task into evidence of how you work.

## What the Interviewer Is Actually Collecting

In a technical screen the interviewer is collecting a process signal, not an answer key. They want to see
that you treat an underspecified prompt as something to clarify, that you state your approach before you
commit to it, that you narrate the tradeoff when you choose a data structure or an error-handling
strategy, and that when something breaks you debug out loud instead of going quiet and guessing. A
correct solution with none of that narration is a weak screen; a candidate who thinks out loud the whole
way, even through a stumble, is the one the interviewer can actually assess.

## Grounded in Your Portfolio, Not Puzzles

The worked tasks in this module are not abstract puzzles. They are real AI-engineering patterns drawn from
the systems you built in the Sans Python portfolio: a token-budget guard, a retrieval relevance floor, a
content guardrail, a subprocess sandbox with a timeout, a read-only kill switch. Each one is a thing a
working AI engineer implements, and each is small enough to code in a screen. You practice the
communication layer on problems that look like the job, not on tricks that do not.

One honest boundary: this module teaches the communication layer and the AI-engineering coding patterns
that show up in real screens. It is not a substitute for dedicated algorithm and data-structure practice.
If your loop includes a pure algorithms screen, drill that separately, as the final module also advises.
What you learn here is how to make any coding round legible to the person evaluating you.

## The Four Lessons

1. **Narrating the Screen** builds the communication layer: clarify before coding, narrate your reasoning,
   think out loud, and avoid the pitfalls that make a screen go quiet.
2. **The Happy Path and the Edges** is implementation discipline: code the working path first, then the
   defensive cases, testing as you go.
3. **Debugging Out Loud** is what to do when you get stuck: a repeatable motion for isolating a bug and
   recovering without a panicked rewrite.
4. **The Clock and the Weak-Screen Audit** is time management under pressure plus the audit you run on
   your own screen performance before the room does.

## What This Module Feeds Forward

By the end you have a coding-screen log in your prep dossier: real screen reps where you recorded the
task, the clarifying questions you asked, the approach you narrated, where you got stuck and how you
recovered, and the tests you ran. That log is the record of the one interview skill that is hardest to
fake and easiest to neglect, and it is yours to keep practicing against right up to the loop.

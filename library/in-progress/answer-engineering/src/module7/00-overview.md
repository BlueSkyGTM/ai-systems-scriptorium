# Module 7: Live Practice and Calibration

You can know the Algorithm cold and still walk into the room uncalibrated. Knowing the method is not the
same as having run it enough times that it fires under pressure, and it is not the same as knowing which
kinds of questions you are actually strong on. This module closes both gaps. It turns the example banks
into a practice loop, and it replaces the feeling of readiness with a count.

Most interview prep ends at "I read the material and I feel ready." That feeling is the least reliable
signal you have. You feel ready on the questions you happened to rehearse, in the categories you enjoy,
on the days you slept well. Calibration is the opposite of a feeling: it is evidence, gathered by
scoring your own reps, that tells you precisely where you are strong and where you are still guessing.

## Practice Is Reps, Not Rereading

A rep is not rereading a lesson. A rep is taking a question you have not rehearsed, running the full
four-step Algorithm on it out loud or on paper, and scoring the result honestly against the rubric. The
distinction matters because rereading builds recognition, and recognition collapses under a novel
question. Reps build the reflex that survives a question you have never seen, which is the only kind the
room will give you that counts.

The example bank you have built across this book is your practice material: the behavioral categories
from the behavioral module, the systems-design prompts from the systems-design module. You do not need
new questions. You need to run the ones you have, again, scoring each rep so the practice is deliberate
rather than a comfortable reread of your best answer.

## The Rubric Is Code

Your self-review rubric in this module is not a checklist you eyeball. It is a script. You log each rep
in your prep dossier with a self-score for each of the four Algorithm steps, and `calibrate.py` reads
those reps and prints a scorecard: how many reps you have logged per signal category, how each step
scores across them, which step is your weakest, which categories are under-practiced, and a single
readiness verdict. The rubric, the scale, and the readiness thresholds live in the code. Your job is to
feed it honest reps and act on what it tells you.

This is deliberate. A prose rubric lets you grade yourself generously without noticing. A scorer that
counts your weak marks and names your thinnest category does not flatter you. It is the same discipline
the rest of the book applies to interview answers, turned on your own preparation.

## The Four Lessons

1. **Deliberate Practice, Not Rote** defines the rep: a fresh question, the full Algorithm, an honest
   self-score, logged.
2. **The Self-Review Rubric** is the scoring schema: the four step dimensions, the strong/partial/weak
   scale, and how to score yourself without inflation.
3. **Reading Your Calibration** runs the scorer and teaches you to act on the scorecard: drill the
   weakest step, fill the under-practiced category, then re-run.
4. **Simulating the Room** is mixed, timed practice across the whole bank, and the readiness verdict that
   tells you when you are prepared and what to drill when you are not.

## What This Module Feeds Forward

By the end you have a deliberate-practice log in your prep dossier and a scorer that grades it. That
scorer is the first dossier artifact that does more than check completeness; it measures quality. The
final module builds on exactly this: it composes your whole dossier, every log and bank and document you
have built, and grades the complete preparation against a rubric in code, the way an interview loop grades
a candidate.
